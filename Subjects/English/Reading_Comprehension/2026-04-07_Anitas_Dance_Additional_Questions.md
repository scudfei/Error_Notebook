## ❌ Question 1: “snob”

**Question:** Jack’s use of the word “snob” to address Anita **best** supports the idea that Anita
* **My Selection (B):** speaks a higher level of English than Jack’s lower class slang
* **✅ Correct Option (A):** **considers herself superior to him and his lower class roots**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Context:** Jack mocks Anita after seeing her house, furniture, and middle-class life with Clive.
* **Evidence:** “You’re coming up and up and up in the world” / “she’s our Educated Woman” / “too good for a man.”
* **Analysis:** Choice B is tempting because Jack’s slang and Anita’s more polished speech do signal class difference. But the word **snob** points beyond speech and toward **status-conscious superiority**. Jack feels judged by Anita’s education, refinement, and social mobility.
* **Conclusion:** This is a classic **partly-true distractor**: language difference is present, but the deeper point is class resentment and perceived superiority.

## ❌ Question 2: “a male dream”

**Question:** The quotation “that dream of cosy domesticity was a male dream” **most strongly** suggests that
* **My Selection (D):** women like Anita’s mother lack the time to dream about all the comforts of home
* **✅ Correct Option (A):** **only men view working to make a comfortable home as a good thing**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Context:** Anita contrasts the fantasy of domestic comfort with her mother’s lived reality of exhausting household labour.
* **Evidence:** Her mother was “anaemic from childbearing and exhausted from scrubbing.”
* **Analysis:** Choice D is not completely unreasonable, but it stays too close to the practical detail. Anita’s point is broader: for men, domesticity looks comforting; for women, it is often relentless labour. The phrase exposes the **gendered asymmetry** behind the fantasy.
* **Conclusion:** The student noticed the burden on women, but stopped short of the stronger ideological point.

## ❌ Question 3: Anita / Nita

**Question:** The writer’s shift in name reference from “Anita” to “Nita” is **best** explained as a
* **My Selection (D):** signal of mood shift between contentment and misery
* **✅ Correct Option (C):** **subtle hint that the childhood character has emerged**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Context:** “Nita” is used when Anita is pulled back into old family dynamics and childhood instincts.
* **Evidence:** The shift appears around Jack’s arrival, memories of her childhood, and the fight scene.
* **Analysis:** Mood does change, so D feels plausible. But the text is doing something more specific: the name shift marks a **reversion to the old self**, the tougher child of that earlier world.
* **Conclusion:** This is a function question, not just a mood question.

## ❌ Question 4: Why university?

**Question:** Anita was **most likely** motivated to go to university to
* **My Selection (A):** allow herself to satisfy her love of literature
* **✅ Correct Option (D):** **avoid the marriage trap that had caught her mother**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Context:** Anita explicitly reflects on her mother’s life and the burdens of domestic expectation.
* **Evidence:** “the real thing she’d lived through … was what had made her so happy to get a scholarship to university.”
* **Analysis:** Choice A uses a real detail: Anita did want to study literature. But the passage makes clear that the *main motive* was escape from the life pattern embodied by her mother. University stands for independence and self-determination.
* **Conclusion:** Again, the student selected a true detail instead of the central motive.

## ❌ Question 5: “genteel phrase”

**Question:** In the quotation, “she had used the genteel phrase for so long,” the “genteel phrase” refers to
* **My Selection (B):** “Born with a plastic spoon?”
* **✅ Correct Option (D):** **“We were…not what you’d call well off”**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Context:** Anita is softening the direct statement that her family was poor.
* **Evidence:** The narrator comments on the phrase immediately after Anita says they were “not what you’d call well off.”
* **Analysis:** This question depends on **grammatical anchoring**. The phrase must refer back to Anita’s own immediately preceding wording, not to Clive’s joke.
* **Conclusion:** The error comes from following the general topic of poverty rather than the exact sentence structure.

## ❌ Question 6: Point of view

**Question:** The point of view from which this story is written is
* **My Selection (B):** omniscient
* **✅ Correct Option (D):** **limited omniscient**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Context:** The narration is third-person, but access to interior thought is centered on Anita.
* **Evidence:** We are repeatedly told Anita’s reflections, but not given the inner thoughts of Jack or Clive except through speech.
* **Analysis:** The student recognized the presence of interior access, but not the limit. Full omniscience would move freely among multiple consciousnesses.
* **Conclusion:** This is a literary-term boundary problem: broad category recognized, subtype missed.

## 📚 Key Vocabulary & Expressions

1. **snob**: a person who looks down on others as socially inferior.
2. **domesticity**: home and family life; household routine.
3. **genteel**: refined or politely indirect, often in an affected way.
4. **limited omniscient**: third-person narration limited mainly to one character’s mind.
5. **inference**: a conclusion drawn from textual clues rather than directly stated.
