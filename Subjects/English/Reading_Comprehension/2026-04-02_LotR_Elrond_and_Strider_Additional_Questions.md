## Overview

This note records two additional reading-comprehension errors from the same Lord of the Rings study session. They are kept separate from the earlier `2026-04-02_LotR_Strider.md` entry because the questions target a different micro-skill set: **best-answer discrimination in literary inference questions**.

### Core Pattern
The student is often attracted to an answer that is **partly true at the surface level**, but not the one that best captures the author’s deeper implication.

---

## ❌ Question 1: Elrond's Description

**Question Focus:** What does the description of Elrond suggest?

**Student's Choice:** **D** (focused on weariness / burden / being tired of immortality)

**✅ Correct Answer:** **A** (Elrond represents historical continuity together with present vitality)

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis

The key wording was effectively:
- **ageless**
- having memory of many things
- **yet hale**
- compared to a **tired warrior**

The student over-weighted the word **tired** and moved too quickly toward a one-directional reading of decline or burden. But the passage is doing something subtler: it presents Elrond as someone who carries the depth of ages while still retaining force and vitality in the present.

So the best answer is not “he is worn down by immortality,” but rather that he embodies **continuity across time**.

### Root Cause
This is a **single-keyword trap**.
The student locked onto one emotionally charged word (“tired”) and ignored the balancing language (“ageless,” “yet hale”).

### Key Takeaway
When a question asks for the **best literary implication**, do not let one vivid word override the full tonal balance of the sentence.

---

## ❌ Question 2: Strider and the Gandalf Comparison

**Question Focus:** What does the comparison to Gandalf suggest about Frodo’s perception of Strider?

**Student's Choice:** **C** (Frodo is admitting former prejudice)

**✅ Correct Answer:** **D** (Frodo senses in Strider a deeper authority / wisdom similar to Gandalf)

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis

Choice C is not totally false. Frodo *is* revising his earlier judgment. But the question asks specifically what the **Gandalf comparison** suggests.

That comparison points beyond mere self-correction. Gandalf represents spiritual depth, authority, hidden power, and trustworthy inner stature. So when Strider reminds Frodo of Gandalf, the passage signals a much deeper shift in Frodo’s perception.

Thus:
- **C** = a partial truth about Frodo’s old bias;
- **D** = the fuller interpretive answer that matches the comparison’s real function.

### Root Cause
This is a **partly-true distractor problem**.
The student identified something valid in the scene, but not the answer that most directly matches the narrative clue.

### Key Takeaway
In literary multiple choice, the wrong option is often **not false** — just **less complete** than the best answer.

---

## 📚 Consolidated Learning Points

1. **Do not over-commit to one loaded word.** Read the whole sentence as a tonal unit.
2. **When a comparison appears, ask what that comparison contributes beyond the obvious.**
3. **Partly-true distractors are dangerous** because they reward shallow recognition without full interpretation.
4. In inference questions, always ask:
   - Which answer is supported?
   - Which answer is *most fully* supported?

---

## ✅ Error Pattern Label

**Pattern:** Partly-True Distractor Selection in Literary Inference

### Common Signs
- the student notices one real feature of the text;
- but chooses an answer that is narrower, flatter, or more literal than the best interpretation.

---

## 🔁 Future Check Routine

Before finalizing an inference answer:
1. Did I base this on the whole phrase, or just one word?
2. Is my answer merely plausible, or does it best explain the author’s choice?
3. Is there a deeper thematic or tonal function I am missing?
4. Am I choosing a **partly true** answer instead of the **best** answer?
