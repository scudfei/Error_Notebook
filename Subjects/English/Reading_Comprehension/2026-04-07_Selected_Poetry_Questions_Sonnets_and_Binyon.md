## ❌ Question 1: Sonnet 130 – “like the sun”

**Question:** The speaker’s comparison of his mistress’ eyes with the sun is called a
* **My Selection (A):** juxtaposition
* **✅ Correct Option (D):** **simile**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** The comparison uses “like,” which makes simile the precise term.
* **Conclusion:** Broad recognition of comparison was present; exact device label was missed.

## ❌ Question 2: On the Future of Poetry – “bomb or two”

**Question:** The phrase “To drop on us a bomb or two?” is an example of which poetic technique?
* **My Selection (B):** metaphor
* **✅ Correct Option (C):** **hyperbole**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** The line uses deliberate exaggeration for comic/rhetorical effect.
* **Conclusion:** Another device-boundary problem.

## ❌ Question 3: On the Future of Poetry – overall attitude

**Question:** The term that **most completely** summarizes the speaker’s feelings about the future of poetry is
* **My Selection (D):** pessimistic
* **✅ Correct Option (B):** **optimistic**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** The speaker admits uncertainty, but the overall poem confidently argues that true poetry will endure. The student overweighted early uncertainty and underweighted the poem’s final confidence.
* **Conclusion:** Whole-poem tone integration remains weak.

## ❌ Question 4: The Burning of the Leaves – except

**Question:** The writer contrasts each of the following related themes **except** for
* **My Selection (A):** summer and fall
* **✅ Correct Option (D):** **chance and destiny**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** Summer/fall, past/present, and death/rebirth all appear in the poem. Chance/destiny does not.
* **Conclusion:** The student recognized one real contrast but missed the “except” logic.

## ❌ Question 5: The Burning of the Leaves – season

**Question:** In which season does this poem take place?
* **My Selection (B):** summer
* **✅ Correct Option (C):** **autumn**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** The “spices of June” refer backward contrastively; the present scene is clearly the burning of fallen leaves in autumn.
* **Conclusion:** A remembered season was mistaken for the present setting.

## ❌ Question 6: The Burning of the Leaves – “to arise...”

**Question:** The phrase “to arise / From squalor of rottenness into the old splendour” is **best** understood to mean that
* **My Selection (A):** in order to blossom, all things must undergo hardships
* **✅ Correct Option (B):** **nature’s new growth is nurtured by decayed old growth**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** A is morally generalized; B stays closely with the poem’s natural cycle of rot feeding renewal.
* **Conclusion:** The student abstracted too loosely away from the text.

## ❌ Question 7: Sonnet 60 – meter

**Question:** This poem is composed using which of the following?
* **My Selection (D):** iambic tetrameter
* **✅ Correct Option (C):** **iambic pentameter**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** Shakespearean sonnets use iambic pentameter: five iambic feet per line.
* **Conclusion:** Poetry mechanics remain unstable.

---

## ❌ Question 8: Keats – who is speaking?

**Question:** The person who is “saying” the words that we read when reading a poem is known as the
* **My Selection (A):** poet
* **✅ Correct Option (C):** **speaker**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** The poet writes the poem, but the voice inside the poem is the speaker.
* **Conclusion:** Literary-role terminology must be kept precise.

---

## ❌ Question 9: Keats – “fair creature of an hour”

**Question:** The main reason why the speaker addresses the “fair creature of an hour” is because
* **My Selection (B):** he would not be able to look at her anymore
* **✅ Correct Option (A):** **the moon is always changing**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** The phrase refers to the moon, figured as a beautiful but temporary/changeable presence. The student chose the nearby emotional consequence instead of the textual referent.
* **Conclusion:** Symbolic reference tracking remains fragile.

## 📚 Key Vocabulary & Expressions

1. **simile**: comparison using “like” or “as.”
2. **hyperbole**: deliberate exaggeration.
3. **speaker**: the speaking voice within a poem.
4. **iambic pentameter**: five iambic feet per line.
5. **contrastive reference**: when a past image is mentioned to sharpen the present one.
