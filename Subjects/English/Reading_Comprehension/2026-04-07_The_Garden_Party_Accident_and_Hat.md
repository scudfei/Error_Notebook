## ❌ Question 1: What is unsettling about Godber’s man’s story?

**Question:** Which statement **best** explains what is unsettling about the story that the man from Godber’s tells?
* **My Selection (A):** A man named Scott had died just outside the gate of Laura’s home.
* **✅ Correct Option (B):** **The man derives pleasure from telling the story about a man’s death.**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Evidence:** “Only Godber’s man seemed to be enjoying himself; it was his story.”
* **Analysis:** A is factually important, but the *unsettling* feature is not the death itself — it is the relish with which he tells it.
* **Conclusion:** The student selected event content instead of narrative attitude.

## ❌ Question 2: “That really was extravagant”

**Question:** As it is used in the phrase “That really was extravagant,” the indefinite pronoun “That” **most likely** refers to
* **My Selection (B):** Jose’s intention of continuing the garden party
* **✅ Correct Option (D):** **Laura’s description of where the man died**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Context:** Laura says the man died “just outside the front gate.” The narrator then corrects that exaggeration.
* **Analysis:** This is a sentence-structure question. “That” points back to Laura’s overstatement about location.
* **Conclusion:** The error came from following topic flow rather than exact referent.

## ❌ Question 3: “eyesore”

**Question:** The **main** effect of describing the cottages as “the greatest possible eyesore” is to
* **My Selection (B):** show what Laura’s family really thought
* **✅ Correct Option (A):** **reveal the elitism of Laura’s family**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** B is partly true, but too shallow. The effect of the description is to expose class prejudice.
* **Conclusion:** The student stayed at reported opinion instead of identifying the function of that opinion.

## ❌ Question 4: Laura will tell Mother

**Question:** Laura’s threat that she will tell their mother about the man having died serves **mainly to** show that
* **My Selection (A):** her main concern is for her mother’s opinion
* **✅ Correct Option (B):** **she has not matured very much since childhood**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Context:** The response echoes an old childhood habit of appealing to Mother.
* **Analysis:** The question is about what this *reveals about Laura*, not about whether she cares for Mother’s opinion in that moment.
* **Conclusion:** The student chose a nearby motive instead of the larger character implication.

---

## ❌ Question 5: Mrs. Sheridan’s response

**Question:** Laura’s mother’s response to the news of the man’s death outside the gate **mainly** indicates that
* **My Selection (D):** her main concern is for her family
* **✅ Correct Option (A):** **she is superficial**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Evidence:** Her strongest reaction is relief that he did not die in *their garden* and therefore not ruin propriety further.
* **Conclusion:** The issue is superficial social concern, not family protectiveness.

---

## ❌ Question 6: “fray”

**Question:** As it is used to describe the genteel and well-heeled garden party, the word “fray” **most strongly** implies that
* **My Selection (D):** chaos and pandemonium break out from drinking too much
* **✅ Correct Option (A):** **the party-goers are not as civilized as they would like to think they are**

### 💡 In-Depth Analysis
* **Analysis:** The word carries ironic pressure: beneath the polite surface is moral disorder.
* **Conclusion:** The student literalized the chaos instead of reading its ironic social implication.

---

## 📚 Key Vocabulary & Expressions

1. **extravagant**: here, exaggerated.
2. **eyesore**: something unpleasant to look at.
3. **elitism**: attitude of superiority toward lower social groups.
4. **superficial**: concerned with appearances rather than depth.
5. **fray**: a conflict or chaotic struggle; here used ironically.
